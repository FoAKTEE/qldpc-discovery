## 1. Motivation

The paper is motivated by the gap between the theoretical promise of quantum LDPC codes and the underexplored finite-length landscape of one specific, near-term-relevant family. Bivariate bicycle (BB) codes (`bravyi2024high`) are attractive because their weight-6 stabilizers and constant-depth syndrome extraction give favorable rate–distance–threshold tradeoffs at practical block lengths `$n \lesssim 1000$`; the `$\code{144,12,12}$` "gross code" reaches a `${\sim}0.7\%$` pseudo-threshold with a `$10\times$` qubit reduction versus the surface code (paper.tex l.64–66). Yet at practical block lengths the BB landscape "remains largely unexplored": all weight-6 codes in `bravyi2024high` have `$k \leq 12$`, later work reached only `$k = 16$` at `$n = 150$` (`wang2024coprime`), and systematic enumerations covered only canonical polynomial forms at small lengths (l.68–70, l.123). Whether higher encoding rates are achievable at weight 6, and at what cost to distance, "has not been systematically investigated" (l.70). A second, methodological motivation: BP-OSD — the standard heuristic distance-bounding tool used in prior BB work — gives only stochastic upper bounds whose reliability for high-rate codes was never quantified against ground truth (l.89–92, l.125–131).

## 2. Goal

The stated goal is to search the large, gradient-free algebraic design space of BB (and a new non-CSS variant) codes for high-quality finite-length representatives, while reliably certifying their parameters and equivalence classes. Concretely the authors evolve *generator ansätze* — Python programs producing candidate polynomial tuples for arbitrary lattice dimensions — rather than individual codes, so that a single mutation can express an algebraic pattern (e.g. "use `$x^{\ell/3}$`") that generalizes across all lattice dimensions and biases the search toward algebraically regular families (paper.tex l.73–76, l.221–222). A coupled goal is to establish a verification standard: ground every distance claim in exact MILP computation and quantify where the customary BP-OSD heuristic fails (l.92, l.104). The four contributions enumerated at l.94–105 are: (i) a reusable LLM-guided pipeline with multi-stage verification; (ii) discovery of 465 distinct codes across five campaigns; (iii) structural characterization of the BB landscape (rate–distance tradeoff, four algebraic families, the `$A=B$` distance-trap theorem); and (iv) systematic quantification of BP-OSD overestimation with a corrective protocol.

- **[ENTRY 2.1]** *(context: search target | claim: evolve generator ansätze `$G(\ell,m)\to\{(A_i,B_i)\}$` (or 4-tuples for PBB) — programs, not codes — so one mutation generalizes across lattices | evidence: `LiteratureGrounded` (l.73–76, l.221–222) | deps: `romera2024funsearch`, `novikov2025alphaevolve`, `openevolve` | status: `checked`)*
- **[ENTRY 2.2]** *(context: declared contributions | claim: four contributions — pipeline, 465-code discovery, landscape characterization, BP-OSD quantification | evidence: `LiteratureGrounded` (l.94–105) | deps: ENTRY 2.1 | status: `checked`)*

## 3. Result scope

At block length `$n \leq 360$` the workflow identifies **465 distinct candidate codes: 97 CSS BB codes and 368 non-CSS perturbed BB (PBB) codes** (abstract l.52; conclusion l.757). The CSS search recovers known high-performing codes and adds new finite-length representatives, including the indecomposable `$\code{288,16,12}$` (`$d=12$` exact, all shifts `$\leq3$`), `$\code{144,8,12}$`, and higher-weight (weight-8) codes up to `$k=50$` at `$d=8$` (l.53, l.98–102, l.762). The non-CSS PBB search produces codes matching the gross code's figure of merit — a `$\code{144,12,12}$` PBB with `$\FOM=12.0$` via mixed X/Z stabilizers — plus the highest trusted non-CSS code `$\code{360,12,{\leq}24}$` (`$\FOM\leq19.2$`) (l.54, l.86, l.99, l.758). Two structural verification outcomes are in scope: the apparent highest-`$k$` `$d=12$` CSS code `$\code{288,24,12}$` is exposed by Tanner-graph analysis as a direct sum of two gross codes and excluded (l.100, l.731); and of the 368 PBB codes, 11 are CSS-equivalent under tested single-qubit Clifford patterns (10 Hadamard, 1 uniform-`$S$` on `$\code{36,4,6}$`), leaving 357 CSS-inequivalent (l.190–194). The empirical scope is explicitly bounded: a rate–distance tradeoff is reported as a regularity *within the searched catalog*, not proven structural (l.78, l.102, l.761).

## 4. Conclusions

The authors conclude that LLM-guided program evolution is a *practical* tool for structured quantum-code discovery when paired with independent verification, achieved here at `${\sim}$US\$400` total cost (abstract l.55; conclusion l.769). The most-emphasized scientific conclusion is an empirical rate–distance tradeoff across the searched weight-6 catalog: high-`$k$` weight-6 codes have `$d\leq4$`, while indecomposable `$d=12$` codes are limited to `$k\leq16$`; weight-8 codes access new `$(k,d)$` points (e.g. `$k=50$` at `$d=8$`) but "do not escape this envelope," and the PBB non-CSS ansatz matches but does not exceed `$\FOM=12.0$` at `$n=144$` — leaving open whether any non-CSS construction over the same ring can escape the envelope (l.761–763). A proven structural conclusion: all BB codes with `$A=B$` and `$k>0$` have `$d=2$` exactly (Theorem `thm:ab_d2`, App. `app:ab_trap`), a distance trap that BP-OSD fails to detect even at `$1.5\times10^6$` trials but that MILP catches in under one second (l.81, l.764, l.959–970). The methodological conclusion: BP-OSD overestimates distance by up to `$12\times$` for high-rate codes and standard syndrome sampling fails entirely on non-CSS codes, so MILP verification plus achievable-syndrome sampling are *essential* for reliable distance claims (l.766). The over-arching design lesson (Discussion l.727): "pipeline design matters more than any single code discovery."

## 5. Key challenge — vast gradient-free combinatorial design space

The central challenge is searching an enormous purely-combinatorial design space with no gradient structure. Every trinomial pair over `$\FF_2[x,y]/(x^\ell{-}1,y^m{-}1)$` defines a valid code, giving `$O(\ell^6 m^6)$` pairs *per lattice* — reduced by symmetries such as translation invariance "but still vast — and no gradient structure amenable to continuous optimization" (paper.tex l.71). The space is also riddled with pathological attractors: the `$A=B$` "distance trap" yields arbitrarily high `$k$` with `$d=2$`, and the evolution *initially converged on it* (l.208–209), so the search must actively avoid degenerate high-`$k$`/low-`$d$` solutions. A further structural difficulty is verifying *distinctness* of discovered codes: code equivalence has no known polynomial-time algorithm and sits near graph isomorphism in complexity (l.389), so even counting genuine results requires BLISS canonical labeling and is reported only as a conservative upper bound (l.393).

## 6. Method innovation — evolve ansatz PROGRAMS, not codes; LLM as mutation operator

The methodological innovation is to evolve *generator ansatz programs* `$G(\ell,m)$` instead of individual codes, using LLMs as mutation operators inside a MAP-Elites loop (OpenEvolve / FunSearch lineage). At each iteration the LLM receives the current highest-fitness ansatz, BB-code domain knowledge, and evaluation feedback, then proposes a *targeted code diff* (exponent adjustments, new strategy branches, control-flow restructuring) rather than a full rewrite (paper.tex l.224–225). The ansatz representation is "key": a single mutation can express an algebraically regular pattern that generalizes across all lattice dimensions, letting one evolved program discover codes at many block lengths simultaneously (l.73–76, l.222). The population is spread over 4–6 islands with migration every 12–25 iterations; the MAP-Elites archive is indexed along two behavioral dimensions — (i) number of lattices yielding `$k\geq8$` codes and (ii) total count of such codes — to reward both breadth and depth and prevent collapse to a single lattice-specific solution (l.226–227). The PBB construction itself is a structural innovation: a non-CSS 4-tuple `$(A,B,C,D)$` with stabilizer matrix `$H=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}A&B&C&D\\0&0&B^\top&A^\top\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$`, rows commuting iff `$(AC^\top+BD^\top)\bmod2$` is symmetric over `$\FF_2$`, reducing to CSS when `$C=D=0$` (l.174–183). Verification machinery (BLISS colored-Tanner-graph dedup, decomposability, the directly-proven Hadamard 2-coloring LC check) is itself a contribution (l.96, l.189–193).

## 7. Possible bottleneck — MILP verification dominates compute; BP-OSD unreliable

The dominant compute cost and central reliability bottleneck is exact MILP distance verification, with BP-OSD too unreliable to substitute. Exact MILP is "prohibitively expensive (`${\sim}10$ min/code`)" inside the loop (paper.tex l.256), so the cascade is engineered specifically to MILP-verify only the `${\sim}400$` surviving codes out of `${\sim}2\times10^5$` candidates (l.258). Individual audits are very heavy: `$\code{288,16,12}$` took 80 min (32 logicals), `$\code{288,24,12}$` 29 min, the gross code 13 min (l.330); deep PBB verification used per-logical timeouts up to 14,400 s with up to 60 workers, and 149 PBB entries underwent deep MILP yielding only 63 exact outcomes (l.327, l.364). The non-CSS symplectic MILP is `${\sim}4\times$` slower per logical, which directly forced Campaign 5 to exclude the large-`$m$` lattices `$(12,12)$` and `$(15,12)$` that had produced the best CSS codes (l.277). The reason MILP cannot simply be replaced: BP-OSD gives only stochastic upper bounds that overestimate by up to `$12\times$` for `$k/n>0.1$`, missed the `$A=B$` `$d=2$` trap even at `$1.5\times10^6$` trials (reporting `$d\leq14$` for a `$\code{144,32,2}$`), and the `$d/\sqrt{n}$` trust filter built on those estimates let the `$\code{360,40,2}$` slip through with `$d_{\rm BP}/\sqrt{n}=1.26$` despite true `$0.11$` (l.211, l.370). For non-CSS codes BP-OSD failed outright (`${\sim}0\%$` decode success) until achievable-syndrome sampling restored it (l.357–360).
